I developped resilience theory during my PhD to study how autonomous systems can handle a partial loss of control authority over their actuators.

After docking to the International Space Station (ISS), the Nauka module suffered a software error causing its thrusters to misfire. In turn, these uncontrolled thrusters rotated the whole space station by 540° before being counteracted by other thrusters of the ISS. Motivated by such a scenario, my PhD thesis investigated the guaranteed resilience of autonomous systems to a similar class of malfunctions called partial loss of control authority over actuators. These malfunctions are characterized by actuators producing uncontrolled and undesirable outputs instead of following the controller’s commands. A loss of control authority can be caused, for instance, by a software bug as in the ISS example or by an adversarial takeover of some actuators of the system.

We consider a system of nominal dynamics $$\dot{x}(t) = f\big(t, x(t)\big) + g\big(t, x(t)\big) \bar{B} \bar{u}(t), \qquad x(0) = x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n,$$ with constant matrix $\bar{B} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times (m+p)}$ and control signal $\bar{u}(t) \in \bar{\mathcal{U}}$.

After a partial loss of control authority, $p$ of the initial $m+p$ actuators of the system are producing uncontrolled and possibly undesirable outputs within their full actuation range. We split $\bar{B}$ into the controlled actuators $B$ and the malfunctioning ones $C$. Similarly, signal $\bar{u}$ is split into the controls $u(t) \in \mathcal{U}$ and the undesirable inputs $w(t) \in \mathcal{W}$. The malfunctioning dynamics become $$\dot{x}(t) = f\big(t, x(t)\big) + g\big(t, x(t)\big) \big(Bu(t) + Cw(t) \big), \qquad x(0) = x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n.$$

This malfunction leads to several problems of interest:

  • can the malfunctioning system resiliently reach a given target?
  • over which actuators can the system resiliently lose control?
  • how to design a system resilient to the loss of control over any one of its actuators?
  • how to quantify the resilience of a system?
I answered all of these questions during my PhD.

Resilient Reachability of Linear Driftless Systems

We started with linear driftless nominal dynamics $\dot{x}(t) = \bar{B} \bar{u}(t)$, with $x(0) = x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n$, and $\bar{u}(t) \in \bar{\mathcal{U}}$. After a partial loss of control authority over actuators, the malfunctioning dynamics become $\dot{x}(t) = Bu(t) + Cw(t)$, $x(0) = x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n$, with control $u(t) \in \mathcal{U}$ and undesirable input $w(t) \in \mathcal{W}$. A target $x_g$ is resiliently reachable if for all undesirable input $w$ there exists a control input $u$ driving the state of the malfunctioning system from $x_0$ to $x_g$. The nominal system is resilient if any target is resiliently reachable. We showed that resilience is equivalent to $BB^\top - CC^\top \succ 0$. This work has been presented at the 2020 IFAC World Congress.

Designing Resilient Linear Systems

With the same linear driftless dynamics as above, we showed that a system in dimension $n$ needs at least $2n+1$ actuators to be resilient to the loss of control over any one of them. We derived a resilient controller $u(t) = B^\top \big( BB^\top \big)^{-1} \big( -Cw(t) + \alpha (x(t) - x_g) \big)$ with $\alpha > 0$ driving asymptotically the state to the target $x_g$. This work has been published in the IEEE Transactions of Automatic Control.

Quantitative Resilience of Linear Driftless Systems

We wanted to quantify how much longer the malfunctioning system needs to reach a target $x_g \in \mathbb{R}^n$ compared to the nominal dynamics, we introduce the nominal reach time $$T_N^*(x_g) := \underset{\bar{u}(t) \, \in \, \bar{\mathcal{U}} }{\inf} \left\{ T : x_g - x_0 = \int_0^T \bar{B} \bar{u}(t) dt \right\},$$ the malfunctioning reach time $$T_M^*(x_g) := \underset{w(t) \, \in \, \mathcal{W} }{\sup} \left\{ \underset{u(t) \, \in \, \mathcal{U} }{\inf} \left\{ T : x_g - x_0 = \int_0^T Bu(t) + Cw(t) dt \right\} \right\},$$ and the quantitative resilience of the system $$r_q := \underset{x_g \, \in \, \mathbb{R}^n}{\inf} \frac{T_N^*(x_g)}{T_M^*(x_g)}.$$ Relying on time-optimal control theory we simplified the expressions of the reach times $T_N^*$ and $T_M^*$. Then, we established the Maximax Minimax Quotient Theorem to solve the nonlinear optimization over $x_g$ in $r_q$. This work has first been presented at the 2021 SIAM Conference on Control and its Applications and a more complete version has been published in the IEEE Transactions of Automatic Control.

The Maximax Minimax Quotient Theorem

The problem of quantifying the resilience of driftless linear systems leads to a nonlinear optimization problem consisting of four nested optimizations : $$\underset{d\, \in\, \mathbb{S}}{\max}\ \frac{\underset{x\, \in\, \mathcal{X},\ y\, \in\, \mathcal{Y}}{\max} \big\{ \|x + y\| : x + y \in \mathbb{R}^+d \big\} }{ \underset{x\, \in\, \mathcal{X}}{\min} \big\{ \underset{y\, \in\, \mathcal{Y}}{\max} \big\{ \|x + y\| : x + y \in \mathbb{R}^+d \big\} \big\} },$$ where $\mathcal{Y} := \big\{ Bu : u \in \mathcal{U} \big\}$ represents the admissible control inputs, while $\mathcal{X} := \big\{ Cw : w \in \mathcal{W} \big\}$ represents the undesirable and uncontrolled inputs. We developed a geometrical solution to this optimization in the case $\dim(\mathcal{X}) = 1$, which is illustrated in the video below generated by our MATLAB code. This work has been published in the Journal of Optimization Theory and Applications.

Resilience of Linear Systems

The nominal dynamics of interest are $\dot{x}(t) = Ax(t) + \bar{B} \bar{u}(t)$, with $x(0) = x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n$, and $\bar{u}(t) \in \bar{\mathcal{U}}$. After a partial loss of control authority over actuators, the malfunctioning dynamics become $\dot{x}(t) = Ax(t) + Bu(t) + Cw(t)$, $x(0) = x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n$, with control $u(t) \in \mathcal{U}$ and undesirable input $w(t) \in \mathcal{W}$. To quantify how much longer the malfunctioning system needs to reach a target $x_g \in \mathbb{R}^n$ compared to the nominal dynamics, we introduce the nominal reach time $$T_N^*(x_0, x_g) := \underset{\bar{u}(t) \, \in \, \bar{\mathcal{U}} }{\inf} \left\{ T \geq 0 : x_g = e^{AT}\left( x_0 + \int_0^T e^{-At}\bar{B} \bar{u}(t) dt\right) \right\},$$ the malfunctioning reach time $$T_M^*(x_0, x_g) := \underset{w(t) \, \in \, \mathcal{W} }{\sup} \left\{ \underset{u(t) \, \in \, \mathcal{U} }{\inf} \left\{ T \geq 0 : x_g = e^{AT}\left( x_0 + \int_0^T e^{-At}\big(Bu(t) + Cw(t) \big)dt \right)\right\} \right\},$$ and the quantitative resilience of the system $$r_q := \underset{x_0 \, \in \, \mathbb{R}^n, \ x_g \, \in \, \mathbb{R}^n}{\inf} \frac{T_N^*(x_0, x_g)}{T_M^*(x_0, x_g)}.$$ Relying on Lyapunov theory we derived analytical bounds on $T_N^*$, $T_M^*$ and $r_q$. This work has first been presented at the 2022 European Control Conference and a more complete version has been published in Automatica.

Resilience of an Orbital Inspection Mission

We demonstrate the resilience of a spacecraft to perform an orbital inspection mission depsite the loss of control authority over one of its thrusters and with a controller further plagued by actuation delay. We extend resilience theory to include resilient trajectory tracking and to account for actuation delays. This work received the John V. Breakwell award at the 2022 AAS/AIAA Astrodynamics Specialists Conference and is submitted to the Journal of Guidance, Control, and Dynamics.
